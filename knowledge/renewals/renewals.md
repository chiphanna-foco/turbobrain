# Renewals

Guide to the Renewal process

## Overview

The goal is a smooth lease renewal process between existing tenants and owners. If an owner or tenant wants to renew, we want to help make that happen. A yearly tenancy is the goal, but a monthly tenancy is better than a turnover from our perspective. Having tenants in place paying rent and owners paying management is the desired outcome.

### Timeline & Key Dates

**210 - 90 days**
- Emails: notifications sent to owners, tenants and ziprent outlining the renewal process and seeing what the plans are for the year ahead. These can be snoozed 5 - 7 days and should be snoozed based on a progressive basis so they do not all reopen on the same day.
- Rent Change: Always double check the laws for each state/county/city and if the property is under rent control for what is allowed. For example, in California, rent increases above 10% must have a 90 day notice plus 7 days for mailing.
- Section 8: Section 8 properties typically require a 60 day notice to be sent to the tenant and to the Housing Authority for the request.
- Lease end: Ending a lease can vary from state to state and city/county. For example, in California, an owner could end a lease that is on a Month to Month basis with 60 day notice plus 7 days for mailing for tenants who have been at a property longer than 1 year.
- MAX INCREASE: We will not issue a rent increase notice for more than 10%. If an owner wants this, they must do it on their own.

**45 days**
- Cut-off for Owner to confirm rent increase for the following month (15th of month)
- EX) If lease expires June 30th, cutoff is May 15th for owner to confirm increase amount
- Rent Change: rent increases under 10% must have a 30 day notice plus 7 days for mailing
- Rent Change: rent increases above 10% must have a 90 day notice plus 7 days for mailing
- Lease end: an owner could end a lease with 30 day notice plus 7 days for mailing for tenants who have been at a property less than 1 year

**37 days**
- Cut-off for tenants to confirm if they intend to renew
- If tenants have not confirmed by 21-25th of month, we will input any rent increases into system

## Property Types

### Houses, Townhouses, and Condos
- Generally not covered by rent control
- Covered by eviction protections in cities such as Berkeley, Oakland and San Francisco
- Owner needs just cause to end the lease in many cities/counties

### Apartment in multi unit buildings
- Generally governed by state rent control and by city rent control if there is one

## Rent Control - California

California has statewide rent control for apartment units capping rent increases in any 12-month period to no more than 5% plus the percentage change in the cost of living (CPI), or 10%, whichever is lower.

### Just Cause Eviction
Most apartments need just cause to end a lease. Houses and condos in select cities need just cause as well. Not signing a lease of like terms, for example another yearly lease before expiration of the current yearly lease, is just cause.

## Oakland Resources

Oakland has a Rent Adjustment Program (RAP) that regulates rent increases for certain rental units, limiting them to 3% annually or 60% of the Consumer Price Index (CPI) increase, whichever is lower, and requiring landlords to provide 30 days' written notice for increases.

In Oakland, most residential buildings constructed before 1983 are covered by rent control, aside from single-family homes and a few other exceptions. When new tenants move into rent-controlled units, the landlord is allowed to set the rent at whatever price they choose.

For Oakland in 2026, the key CPI figure is the 0.8% annual rent increase rate set by the city for August 2025-July 2026. The actual San Francisco-Oakland CPI for late 2025 (Dec 2025) was around 3.0%, showing actual inflation was higher than the rent cap.

**Just Cause Eviction in Oakland**
Per Measure V, which was passed last Nov. 8, the Just Cause for Eviction Ordinance (JCO) now applies to residential rental units in Oakland built before April 1, 2013. Owners of rental units covered under the JCO must allege Just Cause when terminating a tenancy. Expiration of a lease is not just cause.

If the property falls under AB1482, the max rent increase is .8% in Oakland. However, if an increase has not been implemented in the last few years, owners can bank increases at .8% per year up to 5 years.

Owners MUST have the Oakland Residential Rent Adjustment Program Document mailed via certified mail to the tenants anytime a rent increase happens. It should be emailed as well and posted in common areas if multi-unit property.

RAP sheet: (linked resource)

As an FYI, Per State law, if at the end of a fixed-term lease, the landlord continues to accept rent payments, it is presumed the lease is renewed with same terms as original lease under Cal. Civ. Code § 1945. Owner must present tenants with new lease agreement prior to expiration of current lease.

Under Oakland's Tenant Protection Ordinance (TPO), owners must honor the terms of the original/current lease agreement, and may not make unilateral changes without tenant's express written consent.

Per Measure V, owners of rental units covered under the JCO can no longer terminate a tenancy for failure to sign a lease renewal with substantially identical terms as the current, expiring lease.

### Info For Single Family Homes and Condos

You usually do not have full rent control protection if you live in a single family home (a single family home with an illegal in-law unit counts as a 2-unit building) or a condominium and you (and your roommates) moved in on or after January 1, 1996. While these units do not usually have limits on rent increases, they do have "just cause" eviction protection, meaning you can only be evicted for one of the just causes unless the tenant shares the rental unit with their landlord.

Exception: If you moved into a single family home which was vacant because the previous tenant was evicted after a 60 or 30 day eviction notice (a no-fault eviction), then you have full rent control protection.

### Just Causes for Eviction Under the SF Rent Ordinance

For residential units covered under the San Francisco Rent Ordinance, the landlord must have honest intent, without ulterior motive, to evict the tenant with "just cause." The following are the just causes:

1. Nonpayment of rent, habitual late payment, or frequent bounced checks
2. Breach (violation) of a term of the rental agreement that has not been corrected after written notice
3. Nuisance or substantial damage to the unit (waste), or "creating a substantial interference with the comfort, safety, or enjoyment"
4. Illegal use of the unit
5. Termination of the rental agreement and the tenant refuses to execute a written extension for materially the same terms
6. The tenant has refused landlord access to the unit as required by law
7. Unapproved subtenant is the only person remaining in the unit
8. Move-in of the landlord or a close relative
9. Sale of a unit which has been converted to a condo (seniors and permanently disabled tenants cannot be evicted)
10. Demolition or removal of the unit from housing use
11. Capital improvements or rehabilitation with all necessary permits
12. "Substantial rehabilitation" of a building that is essentially uninhabitable
13. Ellis Act evictions (withdrawal from rental housing use)
14. Lead abatement as required by SF Health Code
15. Demolition or removal per development agreement terms
16. Good Samaritan Occupancy Status expiration (within 60 days after expiration)

## Seattle

### Rent Increases - Minimum 180 days notice required

Any rental agreement or renewal for a residential rental unit in The City of Seattle entered into after November 8, 2021 must include a provision requiring at least 180 days' prior written notice whenever periodic or monthly housing costs are to increase (except for subsidized tenancy where notice is 30 days).

It takes two notices to renew a fixed-term rental agreement:
1. Give the 180 day notice of rent increase if new offer will increase rent (if 10%+, follow extra delivery requirements)
2. Between 60 and 90 days before current term ends, serve either notice offering new rental agreement or notice of just cause for refusal to renew

Resident must be allowed minimum 30 days to sign any offered renewal.

If the increase is 10%+ over lowest rate in past 12 months, notice must be delivered by hand-delivery or mailing both certified mail with return receipt and regular US mail. Must also include packet about new economic displacement relocation assistance program.

### Notice to Vacate

If month-to-month tenant, must give notice at least 20 days (not including day notice served) before last day of month you want to move out. For example, to move out by July 31, must give notice no later than July 11.

For rental agreement for a term, read the section on giving notice. Common for term rental agreements to require 30 days advance written notice if intending to stay or move out before term expires.

Landlords are required to offer a tenant in good standing a reasonable lease renewal offer 60-90 days before current lease expires. Tenant then has 30 days to respond.

### Notice of Non-Renewal Not Covered by Just Causes

If owner wants to proceed with notice of non-renewal not following just cause provisions, must proceed with TRAO (Tenant Relocation Assistance Ordinance License) which includes fees of $578.25 and takes approximately 6 months.

The reasons allowed to terminate a month-to-month tenancy or decline to renew a fixed-term lease are restricted to just causes under Seattle Code.

If cause involves substantial rehabilitation, requires obtaining TRAO License prior to issuing notice to vacate.

Starting 1/1/2024, SDCI implemented a TRAO Processing Fee: Base fee of $257 + $321.25 for each unit requiring service of Tenant Information packets.

The tenant relocation (TRAO) process takes approximately 6 months from date of TRAO Application Meeting.

### Just Cause For Eviction

The City of Seattle offers renters modest additional protections. These laws apply to all residential tenancies in Seattle city limits.

In Seattle, landlords must have 1 of 16 "Just Cause reasons" if they want to terminate tenancy. Must give written notice "Notice to Terminate Tenancy" stating specific just cause and supporting facts. The amount of advance notice depends on the specific just cause reason. Generally, notice period is 20 days before end of rental period unless otherwise stated.

**The 16 just causes are:**

1. Late rent: 14 day notice to pay or vacate, failure to comply
2. "Habitual failure": 4+ 14 day pay or vacate notices in most recent 12 months
3. Violation of rental agreement: 10 day notice to comply or vacate, failure to comply
4. "Habitual failure": 3+ 10 day notices to comply or vacate in most recent 12 months
5. Landlord or immediate family member needs to move in (90 day notice)
6. Landlord wants to sell the unit (90 day notice, only applies to single-family dwellings, defined as "detached structures containing one dwelling unit with permanent foundation")
7. Occupancy of unit depends on being employed on property and employment is terminated
8. Landlord rents portion of own home or ADU and no longer wishes to share
9. Landlord wants substantial remodel displacing you permanently (requires city relocation license, ~6 month process)
10. Landlord wants to demolish property or change use to non-residential (requires relocation license)
11. Landlord wants to convert unit to condo or co-op
12. Landlord receives notice of violation for housing standards in permitted ADU and wants to discontinue renting
13. Landlord receives notice of violation for unauthorized/illegal unit and has to discontinue renting (landlord pays $2,000 or 2 months' rent relocation assistance 2 weeks before moveout)
14. Landlord has to reduce number of renters to comply with legal limit (30 day notice, $2,000 or 2 months' rent relocation assistance 2 weeks prior)
15. Landlord issued emergency order to vacate and close unit due to hazardous conditions (varies, very short period)
16. Landlord issues notice for engaging in criminal activity on property (landlord must specify crime and provide copy to City)

Just cause rights cannot be waived. Any rental agreement attempting to state otherwise cannot be enforced.

It is a violation of the Just Cause Eviction Ordinance for a landlord to rely on a just cause reason and fail to follow through. Fines, penalties, and $2,000 in damages apply.

In Washington all notices must be physically issued as well as mailed via certified mail. We issue these physical notices with owner confirmation for $100. Owners may also have option of handling physical noticing. Use ABC legal: collections@ziprent.com

## Seattle

### Rent Increases - Minimum 180 days notice required

Any rental agreement or renewal of a rental agreement for a residential rental unit in The City of Seattle entered into after November 8, 2021 shall include or shall be deemed to include a provision requiring at least 180 days' prior written notice whenever the periodic or monthly housing costs to be charged a tenant are to increase, except that for a subsidized tenancy where the amount of rent is based on the income of the tenant or circumstances specific to the subsidized household, the rental agreement shall instead provide at least 30 days' prior written notice of an increase in the amount of rent to each affected tenant.

It takes two notices to renew a fixed-term rental agreement. First, you must give the 180 day notice of rent increase if your new offer will increase the rent; if the increase is for 10% or more, you must follow the extra delivery requirements. Second, between 60 and 90 days before the current term ends, you must serve either a notice offering the new rental agreement or a notice of which just cause you are using to refuse to renew the tenancy. The resident must be allowed a minimum of 30 days to sign any offered renewal. If the increase is for 10% or more over the lowest rate in the past 12 months, the notice of increase must either be delivered to each adult at the unit by either hand-delivery or mailing both by certified mail with return receipt and by regular US mail. The notice must also include a packet about the new economic displacement relocation assistance program. This packet has not yet been developed by SDCI, but should be available sometime in the next six months.

### Notice to Vacate

If you are a month-to-month tenant, you must give notice to your landlord in writing at least 20 days (not including the day your serve the notice) before the last day of the month that you want to move out. For example, if you wanted to move out by July 31, you must give notice no later than July 11.

If you have a rental agreement for a term, you will need to read the section that provides details on giving notice. It is quite common for a rental agreement for a term to require 30 days advance written notice if you intend to stay or move out before the term expires. Landlords are required to offer a tenant in good standing a reasonable lease renewal offer 60-90 days before the current lease expires. You then have 30 days to respond.

### Notice of Non-Renewal Not Covered by Just Causes

If an owner wants to proceed with a notice of non-renewal that does not follow the just cause provisions, we would need to proceed with a TRAO (Tenant Relocation Assistance Ordinance License) which includes fees of $578.25 and it could take approximately 6 months from the date the fees were paid to get to the TRAO meeting. The reasons allowed to terminate a month-to-month tenancy or decline to renew a fixed-term lease are restricted to just causes under the Seattle Code, a summary of which can be reviewed here: Just Cause Eviction Ordinance - RentinginSeattle | seattle.gov and the full text can be read Chapter 22.205 - JUST CAUSE EVICTION. While there is a cause related to substantial rehabilitation (section H), it requires obtaining a Tenant Relocation Assistance Ordinance License prior to issuing a notice to vacate. You can read more about the ordinance here: Tenant Relocation Assistance Ordinance - SDCI | seattle.gov, and for more information about submitting an application for a Tenant Relocation Assistance Ordinance License, please see: https://buildingconnections.seattle.gov/2023/12/01/sdci-launches-zendesk-for-tenant-relocation-assistance-holds/

Starting 1/1/2024 SDCI implemented a TRAO Processing Fee. This fee is charged to the developer or property owner and is a separate fee from the owner's share of relocation assistance paid to eligible tenants. This fee is used for increased staffing to provide timely and efficient processing of TRAO licenses. The 2024 fee includes a base fee of $257 + $321.25 for each unit that requires service of the Tenant Information packets. You can read more about TRAO Processing fees at Fees - SDCI | seattle.gov. After the TRAO processing fees have been paid, your tenant relocation file will be assigned to an analyst who will contact you to schedule the TRAO Application Meeting. The tenant relocation (TRAO) process will take approximately 6 months from the date of your TRAO Application Meeting.

### Just Cause For Eviction

The City of Seattle offers renters some modest additional protections than in other parts of Washington State. These laws apply to all residential tenancies in the Seattle city limits. If you're unsure whether your unit is located in the city limits of Seattle, you can look it up on the King County Parcel Viewer.

In Seattle, landlords must have 1 of 16 "Just Cause reasons" if they want to terminate your tenancy. Your landlord must give you a written notice commonly called a "Notice to Terminate Tenancy" and state the specific just cause and supporting facts. The amount of advance notice depends on the specific just cause reason. In general, the notice period is 20 days before the end of a rental period unless otherwise stated below. You can always contact the Renting in Seattle helpline at (206) 684-5700 for help to make sure a termination notice is valid and correct. To access the actual ordinance see the Just Cause Eviction Ordinance website.

The 16 just causes your landlord can use to end your month-by-month rental agreement are:

- **Late rent:** you receive a 14 day notice to pay or vacate and fail to comply.
- **"Habitual failure":** you receive 4 or more 14 day pay or vacate notices in the most recent 12 month period for late rent.
- **Violation of your rental agreement:** You receive a 10 day notice to comply with the rules of your rental agreement or vacate and you fail to comply.
- **"Habitual failure":** you have received 3 or more 10 day notices to comply or vacate in the most recent 12 month period for failure to comply with the rules of your rental agreement.
- **Your landlord or a member of their immediate family needs to move into your unit.** This requires a 90 day notice. Your landlord can be required by the City to certify (sign a sworn declaration) if they use this just cause and you suspect they do not intend to occupy your unit when you move out.
- **Your landlord wants to sell the unit you rent.** This requires a 90 day notice and only applies to single-family dwelling units, defined by City code as "detached structures that contain one dwelling unit and have a permanent foundation." If you live in a condo, apartment, duplex, triplex, or townhome, your landlord cannot use this as a just cause reason to end your rental agreement.
- **Your occupancy of a unit depends on being employed on the property and your employment is terminated.** This would typically apply to property managers who live on site.
- **Your landlord rents a portion of their own home or an accessory dwelling unit to their own home and no longer wishes to share with you.**
- **Your landlord wants to substantially remodel your unit or the building where you live displacing you permanently.** This requires your landlord to apply to the City for a relocation license which is approximately a 6-month process. The license requirements include giving you an information packet and paying you relocation assistance if your income is at or below 50 percent of the median income for King County. For more details, read the Tenant Relocation Assistance webpage.
- **Your landlord wants to demolish the property where you live or change the use to non-residential.** This requires a relocation license the same as displacement from a substantial remodel. See above.
- **Your landlord wants to convert your unit to a condo or a co-op.**
- **Your landlord receives a notice of violation for housing standards in a permitted accessory dwelling unit and wants to discontinue renting it.** The landlord must pay you relocation assistance in the amount of $2,000 or the equivalent of 2 months' rent two weeks before you move out.
- **Your landlord receives a notice of violation for an unauthorized housing unit, commonly called an "illegal unit," and has to discontinue renting your unit.** The landlord must pay you relocation assistance of either $2,000 or the equivalent of 2 months' rent 2 weeks before you move out.
- **Your landlord has to reduce the number of renters in a dwelling unit to comply with the legal limit.** This requires a 30-day notice and payment of relocation assistance of $2,000 or the equivalent of 2 months' rent 2 weeks prior to move out.
- **Your landlord is issued an emergency order by the City to vacate and close your housing unit due to hazardous conditions.** The notice requirement depends on the specific circumstances of the emergency, but it is always a very short period of time. You may get relocation assistance if the emergency condition is found to be the landlord's responsibility. Low-income households receive $4,734.
- **Your landlord issues you a notice for engaging in criminal activity on the property.** The landlord must specify the crime and facts supporting the allegation in the notice of termination and provide a copy to the City.

Just cause rights cannot be waived. Any rental agreement that attempts to state otherwise cannot be enforced.

It is a violation of the Just Cause Eviction Ordinance for a landlord to rely on a just cause reason to end a rental agreement and fail to follow through, whether that means not moving into the unit, not listing it for sale, etc. Fines and penalties will apply, and renters have the right to sue for $2,000 in damages.
