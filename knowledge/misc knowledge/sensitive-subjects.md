# Sensitive Subjects

Often times sensitive subjects will come up. We want to make sure to have an empathetic ear and listen as much as possible. Make sure not to promise anything on these calls. You want to make sure that you assure them you will get this in front of the correct manager to make sure it is handled with utmost respect and care. There could be legal aspects that we may need to look into further depending on the location of the property so that is why we want to make sure we are able to listen to what is being requested and get clarity from the individual about how they would like to move forward and what they are seeking in this call or email.

## Domestic Violence

### Definition
Domestic violence is the willful intimidation, physical assault, battery, sexual assault, and/or other abusive behavior as part of a systematic pattern of power and control perpetrated by one intimate partner against another. It includes physical violence, sexual violence, psychological violence, and emotional abuse. The frequency and severity of domestic violence can vary dramatically; however, the one constant component of domestic violence is one partner's consistent efforts to maintain power and control over the other.

### Tenant Rights under Domestic Violence in CA

The law may protect you if you have:
- A restraining order, OR
- A police report showing that you were the victim of domestic violence, sexual assault, human trafficking, stalking, or elder/dependent adult abuse

The police report or restraining order can be no more than 180 days old. Under California law, a tenant can inform their landlord that they were, or a member of their household was, a domestic violence victim. If they submit the proper documentation, they can terminate their lease.

### Landlord Rights (CA)

The California Code of Civil Procedure Section 1161.3 outlines the domestic violence-related circumstances under which a landlord can terminate or opt not to renew a lease. The law says that a landlord neither terminate nor refuse to renew a lease due to an act of domestic violence against the tenant or a member of the tenant's household as long as:
- The domestic violence has been documented by a protective order, a police report, or third-party documentation, as described above; and
- The alleged perpetrator isn't a tenant or household member of the same apartment or house

## Police Called to the Property

If we hear from the tenant, a neighbor or the owner that the police have been called to the property:
- Request the police report from the tenant
- Find out the nature of why the police were called to the property
- If any charges were brought and if the issue is now resolved
- Update the owner on any findings
- If the police came to property for a reason that is against the rules and terms of the lease we can use the police report as cause to start the eviction process

## Third Party Complaints

If a neighbor or any 3rd Party complains about a tenant:
- We cannot release any information about the tenants without the tenants permission to ANY 3rd Party
- Anything that the police should be involved with we need a Police Report in order to move forward
- Take the complaints to the tenant to find out the validity of the claim or complaint
- When reaching out to the tenant, play devil's advocate that the 3rd party could be misleading us for other reasons
- Usually state something in the vein of "We got a concerning report that: " "Can you please let us know what happened" - this usually keeps the tenant feeling protected to discuss
- If this is not the first time we have had this complaint, come back a little stronger with the tenant especially if it is something that is a violation of their lease and an eviction-able offense
- Inform the owner of the claim as well
- Find out if the owner has connections within the neighborhood to get more information of the 3rd party complaining or verify or counter the claim
